Deep Hedging via Generative Adversarial Networks and Fractional Brownian Noise: A Comprehensive Project Plan for Overcoming Regime FragilityIntroduction to the Evolving Hedging ParadigmThe discipline of quantitative finance has historically relied on continuous-time stochastic calculus and partial differential equations (PDEs) to derive optimal hedging strategies. Rooted in the Black-Scholes-Merton paradigm, conventional dynamic hedging assumes frictionless markets, continuous trading, and geometric Brownian motion as the underlying stochastic driver. However, the theoretical elegance of continuous martingales sharply diverges from the empirical reality of financial markets. Frictions such as transaction costs, bid-ask spreads, and discrete trading intervals inherently invalidate the assumption of perfect replication. In response to these structural limitations, deep hedging has emerged as a revolutionary, purely data-driven paradigm. By reformulating the derivative hedging problem as a sequential Markov decision process, reinforcement learning (RL) agents can autonomously discover optimal execution protocols without relying on predefined parametric pricing models.In controlled, synthetic environments characterized by classical stochastic volatility models, deep reinforcement learning agents routinely outperform traditional Black-Scholes delta hedging. These agents successfully minimize hedging costs and optimize modern risk measures, such as Expected Shortfall and Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR), by systematically navigating transaction costs and liquidity constraints. Yet, the transition from synthetic, mathematically pristine continuous-time martingales to raw, empirical market data has exposed profound vulnerabilities within these purely data-driven architectures. Empirical deep hedging models systematically underperform when exposed to out-of-sample structural breaks, real-world memory effects, and regime fragility.When trained on historical datasets—such as multi-year panels of S&P 500 index options—these agents inevitably overfit to the prevailing macroeconomic conditions that dominate the training sample. They learn to execute systematic "delta haircuts," an intentional underhedging strategy that exploits the standard inverse correlation between equity spot prices and implied volatility. While this haircut provides superior accumulated rewards during standard market operations, it renders the neural policy catastrophically fragile during adverse, "rough" volatility regimes where the standard spot-volatility channel breaks down entirely.To engineer a hedging framework that retains the non-parametric adaptability of reinforcement learning while enforcing rigorous mathematical robustness against out-of-sample micro-regimes, the training environment itself must be fundamentally re-architected. This report details an exhaustive, literature-backed project plan to resolve empirical regime fragility. The proposed methodology leverages a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) strictly conditioned on a Markov approximation of fractional Brownian motion (MA-fBM). By generating infinite, synthetic, non-Markovian market regimes constrained to the "rough" volatility space (where the Hurst parameter $H < 0.5$), the generative model forces the reinforcement learning agent to learn jump-resistant, memory-aware execution protocols. The resulting architecture—culminating in the symbolic distillation of the neural policy into auditable, closed-form mathematics—provides a highly profitable execution protocol that thrives precisely where classical delta hedging and naive empirical RL models completely collapse.The Pathology of Empirical Deep Hedging and Regime FragilityTo construct a mathematically robust deep hedging engine, it is necessary to thoroughly dissect the mechanics of how and why current state-of-the-art empirical deep hedging agents fail in out-of-sample walk-forward tests. The standard empirical approach to deep hedging treats the risk management problem as a discrete-time control task, typically solved using continuous-action actor-critic architectures such as the Twin Delayed Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (TD3) algorithm.The Local Downside-Shortfall Reward MechanismTraditional quantitative hedging seeks to minimize terminal hedging error variance. Deep hedging, conversely, is frequently engineered to optimize asymmetric, path-wise downside risk. This aligns with the practical realities of institutional market making, where daily margin calls and regulatory capital constraints render large, transient drawdowns unacceptable, even if the terminal payout is theoretically sound.A standard empirical implementation of this objective utilizes a local downside-shortfall reward, which immediately penalizes negative daily hedging profit and loss (P&L) without permitting subsequent positive daily P&L to offset the penalty symmetrically. A formalized mathematical representation of this reward function, empirically tested on S&P 500 index options, is defined as:$$r_{t+1} = 10 \left( 0.03 + PnL_{t+1}^{(100)} - \kappa \left| PnL_{t+1}^{(100)} \right|^\alpha \right)$$In this equation, $PnL_{t+1}^{(100)}$ represents the daily P&L multiplied by 100, the parameter $\kappa$ operates as a penalty multiplier strictly applied to downside deviations, and $\alpha$ dictates the polynomial scaling of the loss. Typically, an absolute-value penalty ($\kappa = 1, \alpha = 1$) is selected over a quadratic penalty to provide a stable, non-explosive gradient signal during neural network training. Because the criterion evaluates performance path-wise, the trained RL agent functions as a learned local shortfall hedger rather than a classical terminal mean-variance optimizer.The Mechanics of the Delta HaircutWhen an agent is subjected to this local downside-shortfall reward using historical market data, the policy network converges on a highly specific structural maneuver: the systematic delta haircut. Instead of holding the mathematically mandated Black-Scholes short position in the underlying asset to neutralize directional risk, the agent intentionally holds a smaller short position, maintaining a positive residual delta exposure.This behavior is not an algorithmic error; it is a highly optimized exploitation of the empirical leverage effect. In a standard market regime, equity spot prices and implied volatility exhibit strong negative correlation. When the spot index rises, implied volatility systematically falls, suppressing the value of the long call option. By maintaining a positive residual delta, the agent's underhedged portfolio reaps directional profits from the spot up-move, which offset the volatility-driven depreciation of the option more efficiently than a perfectly neutralized Black-Scholes hedge. The delta haircut essentially functions as a structural statistical arbitrage embedded within the risk measure's optimization surface.The Manifestation of Regime FragilityWhile the systematic delta haircut appears mathematically optimal within the distribution of the training data, its reliance on the persistence of the spot-implied-volatility co-movement introduces severe regime fragility. To mathematically inoculate the agent against regime fragility, the training environment must be expanded via generative models to include synthetic "rough" volatility regimes where these standard correlations structurally collapse.Fractional Brownian Motion and the Rough Volatility ParadigmTo synthesize market environments that force the reinforcement learning agent to abandon its fragile delta haircut, the underlying generative noise must accurately reflect the anti-persistent, heavy-tailed properties of market panics. Standard continuous-time models rely on standard Brownian motion (BM), a stochastic process characterized by independent increments, continuous sample paths, and light-tailed normal distributions. Because the increments are independent, BM possesses no long-term memory, fundamentally misaligning with the empirical realities of volatility clustering and long-range dependencies observed in financial time series.Empirical volatility surfaces and high-frequency data consistently demonstrate that volatility is "rough". To capture this phenomenon mathematically, modern stochastic volatility frameworks, such as the rough Bergomi (rBergomi) model, replace standard Brownian drivers with fractional Brownian motion (fBM).The Hurst Parameter and Anti-PersistenceFractional Brownian motion is uniquely defined by its Hurst parameter, $H \in (0, 1)$, which governs the covariance structure and the roughness of the sample paths. When $H = 0.5$, fBM collapses into standard Brownian motion with zero correlation between increments. When $H > 0.5$, the increments exhibit positive correlation, resulting in long-term memory, trend-reinforcing persistence, and smoother diffusion paths.However, empirical financial data overwhelmingly dictates that $H < 0.5$. In this regime, the increments are negatively correlated, meaning a positive move is highly likely to be followed by a negative move, and vice versa. This anti-persistence generates highly erratic, "rough" sample paths. The lower the Hurst parameter (e.g., $H = 0.1$), the rougher the path, mimicking the explosive, jump-like behaviors observed during flash crashes and structural market breaks.When a deep hedging framework is exposed to rough Bergomi paths with a Hurst parameter approaching zero, the hedging P&L distribution exhibits extreme left-skewness, characterized by a heavy left tail indicating severe systemic losses. This behavior closely mirrors the catastrophic drawdowns seen when delta hedging under jump diffusion models, even though the rough price process remains a continuous martingale. By explicitly forcing the generative model to sample from an $H < 0.5$ distribution, the reinforcement learning agent will be bombarded with adverse, broken-correlation states, neutralizing its tendency to rely on the fragile empirical leverage effect.Overcoming Non-Markovianity via Markov Approximation (MA-fBM)While fractional Brownian motion provides the precise statistical topology required to simulate regime breaks, deploying it within a deep learning framework presents a severe mathematical hurdle. Neural stochastic differential equations (Neural SDEs), continuous-time score-based generative models, and standard reinforcement learning environments fundamentally rely on the Markov property.Because the increments of fBM are inherently correlated for any $H \neq 0.5$, the process is strongly non-Markovian and is not a semimartingale. Consequently, classical stochastic control techniques fail, and the Kolmogorov (Fokker-Planck) equations cannot be invoked to derive the reverse-time models necessary for generative diffusion. Direct numerical simulation of fBM via Cholesky decomposition or the Davies-Harte method scales poorly and cannot be seamlessly integrated into auto-differentiable neural network training loops.The Infinite-Dimensional Markovian RepresentationTo bypass this non-Markovian barrier without sacrificing the heavy-tailed, anti-persistent memory structures of rough paths, this project will employ a highly advanced technique from stochastic analysis: the Markov approximation of fractional Brownian motion (MA-fBM). The theoretical foundation of MA-fBM dictates that the non-Markovian fractional process can be reconstructed with arbitrary precision as a linear superposition of a finite number of Markovian semimartingales—specifically, Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) processes.Let $K \in \mathbb{N}$ denote the number of augmenting OU processes utilized in the approximation. Even a relatively small number of processes (e.g., $K = 10$) has been proven empirically sufficient to capture the long-term dependencies of fBM. The dynamics of the $k$-th OU process, $Y_t^{(k)}$, driven by a standard Brownian motion $B_s$, are defined as:$$Y_t^{(k)} = \int_0^t e^{-\gamma_k(t-s)} dB_s, \quad k \in \{1, \dots, K\}, \quad t \ge 0$$Here, $\gamma_k$ represents the speed of mean reversion for the specific process. To capture dynamics across multiple temporal scales, these speeds are explicitly distributed across a geometrically spaced grid: $\gamma_k = r^{k-n}$, where the base $r > 1$ and the centering parameter $n = \frac{K+1}{2}$.L2-Optimal Approximation Coefficients and Covariance EquationsThe composite MA-fBM process, denoted $\hat{B}_t^H$, is subsequently constructed as the weighted linear combination of these independent OU processes:$$\hat{B}_t^H = \sum_{k=1}^K \omega_k Y_t^{(k)}, \quad H \in (0, 1), \quad t \ge 0$$where $\omega_k \in \mathbb{R}$ represent the critical approximation coefficients. For the synthetic data to be statistically indistinguishable from a true rough regime, these weights must be rigorously optimized to minimize the $L^2(\mathbb{P})$-error against the theoretical fBM process over a defined terminal time $T$.This optimization reduces to finding the global minimum of a quadratic form, yielding a closed-form linear algebraic system defined as:$$A \omega = b$$The precise structure of the covariance matrix $A$ and the vector $b$ varies depending on whether a Type I or Type II approximation of fBM is implemented. For the standard Type I approximation, the elements are mathematically derived from the variance evolution as follows :$$A_{i,j}^{(I)} = \frac{2T + \frac{e^{-\gamma_i T} - 1}{\gamma_i} + \frac{e^{-\gamma_j T} - 1}{\gamma_j}}{\gamma_i + \gamma_j}$$$$b_k^{(I)} = 2T \gamma_k^{H-1/2} - T^{H+1/2}\gamma_k \frac{\Gamma(H+3/2)}{\gamma_k^{H+3/2}} + e^{-\gamma_k T} - Q(H+1/2, \gamma_k T)e^{\gamma_k T}\gamma_k^{H-3/2}$$where $\Gamma(z)$ represents the standard gamma function, and $Q(z, x)$ denotes the regularized upper incomplete gamma function. By explicitly solving this linear system, the resulting MA-fBM provides a fully tractable, highly parallelizable, and auto-differentiable Markovian reference process. This mathematical bridge allows the generative neural networks to inject extreme rough volatility directly into the training environment while maintaining the computational efficiency required for millions of RL training episodes.Synthetic Market Generation via Conditional Signature GANsWhile MA-fBM provides the underlying topological noise required for rough regimes, raw stochastic noise must be intelligently transformed into realistic, multi-dimensional financial variables to serve as a functional training environment. The RL agent requires a coherent interplay between equity spot prices, the term structure of implied volatility, and option moneyness. Standard sequence generation architectures, such as standard recurrent networks, TimeGAN, or feed-forward generator-discriminator pairs, are theoretically insufficient. The SigGAN framework operates by leveraging the mathematically rigorous theory of rough paths.The defining innovation of the SigGAN is its use of the "signature" of a path. The signature is a universal, non-commutative feature set that effectively extracts the geometric and temporal characteristics of a sequential path, mapping the infinite-dimensional time series into a graded tensor algebra. By employing path signatures as the core embedding mechanism, the GAN's discriminator network can flawlessly evaluate the realism of the synthetic rough time series by comparing the extracted path characteristics against historical market data. This mechanism uniquely characterizes any continuous function and completely eliminates the need to hand-craft specific financial pricing heuristics into the discriminator.The generator network within the SigGAN utilizes a Conditional Auto-Regressive Feed-forward Neural Network (AR-FNN). The AR-FNN is a specialized architecture explicitly dedicated to learning sequence structures. Crucially, instead of seeding the generator with classical uncorrelated Gaussian noise, the AR-FNN will be strictly conditioned on the multivariate MA-fBM paths (where $H < 0.5$) generated via the optimized covariance matrices detailed in the previous section. This guarantees that the AR-FNN transforms the underlying rough noise into synthetic spot and volatility surfaces that inherit the mathematically guaranteed jump-like memory effects.Causal Optimal Transport and Arbitrage ConstraintsTo ensure that the generated multi-dimensional data preserves critical non-linear co-dependence relationships—such as the asymmetric tail dependence observed during market crashes where massive price drops correlate with exponential volume spikes—the discriminator's loss function must penalize non-causal transport plans. Borrowing structural elements from Causal Optimal Transport GANs (COTGAN), the project will heavily augment the traditional Wasserstein distance-based loss function.In a standard optimal transport formulation, there is no restriction preventing the generated data at time $t$ from being influenced by the noise realization at time $t+1$. In financial time series, this violates the fundamental arrow of time and creates artificial look-ahead bias, leading to arbitrageable data arrays. To strictly enforce causality, the classical transport cost $c(x,y)$ between the real data distribution $x$ and the synthetic distribution $y$ is constrained by an added penalty term parameterized by two specific neural networks, $h$ and $M$ :$$c_\phi^K(x,y) = c(x,y) + \sum_{j=1}^J \sum_{t=1}^{T-1} h_{\phi_1, t}^j(y) \Delta_{t+1} M_{\phi_2, t}^j(x)$$In this adapted Wasserstein formulation, the networks $h$ and $M$ (parameterized by $\phi_1$ and $\phi_2$) are simultaneously trained to maximize the cost whenever the generator attempts to construct non-causal transport plans. By minimizing this heavily penalized distance, the SigGAN guarantees that the generated rough regimes are temporally consistent, causality-preserving, and mathematically arbitrage-free.
